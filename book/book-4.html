Chapter IV

The Poisoned Heart

Age of Peoples · Years 54 - 57

There was an Elf named Thrallian. This Book will say his name plainly, once, because everything he became began with the fact that he was, first, only a person - and the cruelest thing Izanami ever did was to prove that anyone at all can be a door, if the grief on the other side pushes hard enough.

He was not wicked. The chronicles are careful on this point, and so is this one. He was gifted, and proud of it, and quietly certain that the long peace of the Elves had never given his gifts anything worthy to test them against. He wanted to matter in a way his people's slow immortal contentment had never asked him to. That was all. That was the door - not hatred, not cruelty, but the ordinary human-shaped wish, in an immortal heart, to be more than he had been allowed to be.

Izanami had been listening for exactly that wish for forty years.

The whisper in the border-dark

She found him where the Elves' forest brushed against one of the thin places of the world, one of those seams where Yomi's cold leaks up on moonless nights. Tsukuyomi's people had learned to fear such places and to keep away from them. Thrallian went closer. He told himself he went to study the cold, to understand it, to be the one Elf brave enough to look. In truth he went because something there had begun, very gently, to tell him he was right about himself.

She never once lied to him. That is what made it work. She only ever told him things he already believed, in a voice that finally agreed.

She did not command him. She could not; the promise she had never made still bought her nothing but the road through longing. She could only offer, and let him take. She offered him understanding. She offered him the certainty that the world had wasted him. And when he had taken enough of that to be hers, she offered him power - real power, the kind his gifted, slighted heart had always felt it deserved - and told him only that it would cost him a little of what he was.

He agreed to the little. The little became more. The chronicles draw a curtain here, and this Book will keep it drawn, for what was done to Thrallian and what Thrallian, unmade, then did, belong to the darkness that made them. It is enough to say that captivity was involved, and cruelty, and a hatred that had to be manufactured because it did not occur in nature - that the first evil of this kind in all of Sekai had to be built, deliberately, out of a person who had merely wanted to be great.

What came out of the wound

Out of that long wrongness, in the fifty-fourth year of the world and the years that followed, came the first of the Orcs. They were not born as the peoples were born, out of love or craft or warmth. They were born out of a wound that Izanami kept open on purpose, shaped by the pain around their making into creatures who knew hatred before they knew anything else - because hatred was the first and only thing the world had shown them.

Two of them stood taller and crueler and cleverer than the rest, and Izanami marked these two for command. The first she called Mugdul. The second she called Sharog. They were her generals before they were fully grown, and they were loyal to her the way a flame is loyal to the hand that will not stop feeding it - which is to say, not at all, and utterly, and only until something larger comes to burn.

And here is the tragedy the tower is built to bury, the thing that Floor III of Izanami's tower exists to force the living to remember: the Orcs were victims first. They were the crime before they were the criminals. Every atrocity they would go on to commit in the wars to come was real, and cost real lives, and must be answered for - and also, the Orcs themselves were the very first thing Izanami's grief destroyed in the land of the living. They were made out of a broken person to break the world. It is possible to hold both of these truths. Sekai would spend a hundred years learning how, and would only truly manage it when an Orc named Lugdum, long after, chose to stand on the right side of a war his whole race had been created to lose.

But that reckoning was generations away. For now there was only the wound, and what had crawled out of it, and a death-goddess in the dark finally holding something in the living world that answered to her. She had made a weapon out of grief. She pointed it at the brightest of the three lights her husband's children had lit.

She pointed it at Ashihara.
