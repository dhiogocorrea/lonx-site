Chapter I

The First Grief

Age of Gods · Years 0 - 1

Before there were floors to climb, before there were swords to fear, before there was even a word for death, there were two. Their names were Izanagi and Izanami, and the world that would one day be called Sekai lay between them like an unfinished song.

They did not find the world so much as agree upon it. Where Izanagi imagined a coastline, Izanami answered with a tide to break against it. Where she dreamed of mountains, he hung the mist that would soften their shoulders at dawn. They worked the way lovers work when the work is also a conversation, and for a long age there was nothing in all of creation but the two of them and the things they made to please one another.

They made the islands and the seas around them. They made the first rivers, and taught them to find the ocean. They made the seasons and set them turning, and when they were pleased with the turning they made living things to feel it - the small green patience of forests, the restless silver of fish, the birds that would carry the morning from one shore to the next. Sekai was young and it was whole, and its makers loved it without measure, which is a dangerous way for anyone to love anything.

The wound in the making

No song is finished without a note that costs the singer something. What that note was, the chronicles do not agree, and this Book will not pretend to a certainty the gods themselves refused to give. Some say the world's last and most beautiful making turned in Izanami's hands and burned her. Others say that to give Sekai the fire it needed to live, she had to carry that fire out of herself, and it did not leave gently.

What is certain is this: in the completing of the world, Izanami was undone by it. The same act that gave Sekai its warmth took the warmth from her. And when she fell, she did not fall into any place her husband could follow by walking. She fell out of the world entirely, downward, into the cold country that had no name until she arrived to give it one.

They would call it Yomi. The land of the dead. It had not existed before there was a death to require it; Izanami's leaving made the room, and then the room kept her.

He had made a world so that nothing he loved would ever have to end. He had not thought to ask what would happen the first time something did.

The descent

Izanagi did what any maker does when a thing he loves breaks: he refused it. He would not let Yomi keep her. He went down into the dark after her, past the roots of the mountains they had raised together, into corridors that grew colder and older with every step, calling her name into a silence that had never had to answer anyone before.

And she answered. From somewhere just beyond his sight she told him she was glad he had come, and sorry, and that he had come too late - that she had already tasted the food of that country, and one who eats in Yomi belongs to Yomi. She asked him to wait, and not to look, while she begged its powers to let her leave.

He waited as long as love could wait, which is never quite long enough. Then he looked. What he saw in the dark of Yomi is the one thing this Book will keep, as he could not: the understanding that the woman he had crossed the underworld to save was no longer only the woman he remembered. Death had made her something else while his back was turned. Grief will do that, given a country to do it in.

He fled. He climbed toward the light he had left behind, and she came after him - no longer asking, now, but pursuing, because to be seen unready and unwilling is its own particular wound, and gods do not forgive small wounds the way mortals learn to. At the mouth of Yomi, where the dark met the first grey promise of the world, Izanagi sealed the way with a stone no living thing could move.

Through it, she made him a promise. If he would keep the door shut against her, she would take from his beloved world a portion of the living, every day, forever - that he might learn what it was to lose, over and over, the way she now would. And through it he made her an answer: that for each one she took, he would call more into being, so that the world would always be fuller of life than of death.

It was, the chronicles say, the first bargain ever struck. It has never been unmade. Everything that comes after in this Book - every war, every blade, every tower - is only the two of them, still keeping it.

❦ ❧

So Izanagi returned alone to the surface of the world, and Izanami remained below. But a thing worth noting, before the light returns and the next chapter with it: she did not hate him yet. Not truly. In that first age she still called it love, and the terrible thing about Izanami - the thing the whole tower is built to hide - is that in some cold and buried way, she never entirely stopped.
