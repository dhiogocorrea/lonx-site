Chapter VI

The Ambition of Sune

Age of the Sword · Years 151 - 154

Nearly thirty years of peace lay over Sekai, and in that peace the world had almost let itself forget the dark. The children of the refugees had children of their own who had never seen Ashihara burn. The Alliance held, more out of habit now than fear. And Izanami, who does not forget and does not tire, spent those years on a single patient discovery: she learned that hatred, gathered thick enough near one of the thin places of the world, could do what she herself could not. It could carry a thing through. Not her - never her, the stone still held - but a small thing. A seed of the underworld, sent up into the light.

She chose her seed with care. Deep in the memory of the world, older than the peoples, there lay the tooth of a dragon - one of the great first beasts, dead so long that its very bones had turned to something between stone and sorrow. A dragon's tooth holds a dragon's nature: pride, hunger, and the particular fire that does not warm but consumes. It was, Izanami saw, the perfect vessel. It only had to be found by the right hands, and then given to the wrong ones.

Trusius, who found the tooth

The one who found it was named Trusius, and of all the figures in this Book he is perhaps the most ordinary, which is precisely why Izanami used him. He was no hero and no villain. He was a man who went where a whisper told him to go and dug where a dream told him to dig, and told himself the whole time that it was luck, and instinct, and his own cleverness. He found the dragon's tooth exactly where Izanami had spent years arranging for him to find it. And then the same whispers that had led him to it led him onward, to the one person in all of Sekai whose heart held the door she needed.

Trusius did not understand what he was carrying. He never did. He believed, right up until the end, that he had simply had the good fortune to bring a remarkable material to a remarkable craftsman. In a way it is the saddest role in the whole history - to be the hand that delivers the ruin of the world and never once know you held it. His remnant lingers still, they say, bound to the forge-floor of the tower, muttering about a piece of luck that would not stop turning in his hands.

Sune, who dreamed of more

The craftsman was called Sune. He was a forger, and by every account the finest of his age - which was exactly the problem, for a man can be the finest of his age and still lie awake certain that the age is too small for him. Sune made blades that kings wore and heroes carried, and he watched the kings and the heroes ride off into the songs, and no song ever mentioned the man who had made the sword. He wanted to matter. He wanted, in the end, the same thing Thrallian had wanted a hundred years before: to be more than the world had allowed him to be.

Izanami never needed to create ambition in anyone. She only needed to find it, already burning, and offer it more air.

When Trusius brought him the dragon's tooth, Sune felt it the way a drowning man feels a rope. Here at last was a material worthy of him - a thing no other forger in the world had ever touched, that would let him make not just the finest blade of the age but the finest blade there had ever been or would ever be. He did not ask where it had come from. He did not ask why the tooth seemed, in the dark of the forge, to be faintly warm. He asked only whether his hands were good enough, and answered himself: yes. Yes, they were. They always had been. The world had simply never given them anything worthy until now.

The forging of LONX

He worked the tooth for three years. The chronicles say the forge never went cold in all that time, that Sune slept beside it and ate beside it and spoke to it, at first to himself and then, increasingly, to something that had begun to speak back. He told himself the voice was inspiration. Every maker knows the feeling of a work that seems to guide the hand - and Sune, who had wanted so long to be guided toward greatness, did not question a voice that told him he was great.

The voice was Izanami's. The tooth was her road, and the forge was her door, and Sune's own hunger was the hand that held it open a little wider with every blow of the hammer. He was not being deceived, exactly. That is the horror of it. He was being agreed with, endlessly, by the only listener who had ever thought him as important as he thought himself.

What came off the anvil at the end of the third year was a sword of terrible and undeniable beauty. Sune named it LONX. It was beautiful enough to be worshipped and dangerous enough to be heard, and the first thing it said, once it was whole and Sune first closed his hand around the grip, was that he had been right about himself all along - and that greatness such as his should not have to serve kings and heroes any longer. It was time the kings and heroes served him.

❦ ❧

Sune listened. Of course he listened; the voice was his own ambition given an edge and a will, and no man has ever easily refused that. He did not yet know that the blade was a mouth, and that mouths must be fed, and that the price of every word LONX would ever speak to him was a soul it would take from the world to pay for it.

He would learn. And by the time he learned, there would already be an army of the taken standing behind him, and the second great grief of Sekai would already be underway.
