Chapter VII

The Phantom Legion

Age of the Sword · Years 156 - 158

A sword that speaks must be fed, and the food of LONX was souls. Sune learned this the first time he used the blade in anger - and to his lasting damnation, he did not stop. For when LONX took a life, it did not merely end that life. It kept it. The soul went into the sword and made it stronger, made its voice clearer, made Sune himself feel more like the great figure the blade insisted he already was. And the taken did not simply vanish. They rose again, changed - spectral, hollow, bound to the will that held the sword.

These were the first Phantoms. This Book must be careful here, because the Phantoms are not the villains of it, however they began. They did not choose to be made. They were, like the Orcs before them, a people forged out of someone else's crime - conscripted out of death itself, given a shape and a hunger and a master, and set marching before any of them had the chance to ask what they were now, or whether they wished to be it. Every Phantom in the legion had been, moments before, a living person with a name. LONX ate the name and kept the strength. What was left obeyed.

The Second Great War

With a legion that grew larger every time it killed - for the fallen simply changed sides - Sune became, in a handful of years, the most dangerous force the world had faced since the Orc Legion. And where the Orcs had been driven by a borrowed hatred, Sune was driven by something harder to fight: the total, radiant certainty that he was in the right. He did not believe he was conquering Sekai. He believed he was correcting it. He believed the world had wasted its greatest maker for too long, and that he was simply, at last, taking the place he had always deserved.

He struck first at Ashihara - rebuilt now, proud again, a full generation removed from its first burning - and then at the island of Unshu, the Dwarven forge-home that had sailed to the mainland's rescue in the last war. The symbolism was not lost on anyone. Sune, a maker scorned, went first for the two greatest works of the two peoples who made things: the Human city and the Dwarven island. Whatever the sword whispered about correction, there was a plainer thing underneath it - the oldest grievance of the overlooked craftsman, turned into an army and pointed at everything that had ever been celebrated instead of him.

He told himself he fought for greatness. He fought, in truth, for the applause he had never been given - and the sword was glad to let him think the two were the same.

The betrayal at Tsurui Forest

Here the war takes a turn that would shape everything after, and it turns on the Orcs. In the long years since the First Great War, the scattered Orcs had not stayed still. Under a leader named Zeakgul, some of them had begun the slow, bitter work of becoming something other than Izanami's abandoned weapon - not good, not yet, but their own. When Sune's Phantom Legion swept toward the border, Sune sought the Orcs as allies, reasoning that a people made of hatred would make fine soldiers for a war of correction.

He was wrong, and the wrongness cost him everything in the end. At Tsurui Forest, Sune and the Orcs came to blows - the chronicles say Sune betrayed Zeakgul, though whether the betrayal was Sune's cold calculation or the sword's hunger reaching for Orc souls the way it reached for all others, none can say. What is certain is that the Orcs, who had been created to serve the darkness, chose in that forest to refuse it a second time. Zeakgul fell. But out of the ruin of his following rose a successor named Lugdum, and Lugdum made the choice that redeems his whole people in the songs that came after.

He led the Orcs into the Triangle Alliance. The race that Izanami had made to destroy Sekai marched, at last, to defend it. There is no moment in all of this history that Izanami hated more, and none this Book loves better. The weapon had turned in the hand. The wound had chosen, against everything it was made to be, to try to heal.

Half Orcs, and the widening of the Alliance

The war made strange kinships, as long wars do. In the years of fighting alongside the peoples they had once been made to slaughter, the Orcs became, slowly, part of the world rather than its enemy - and from that mingling came the Half Orcs, born between the reconciled clans and the human towns that had learned, warily, to shelter them. They were rejected at first by both sides, belonging fully to neither, and they became for that very reason the truest bridge the Alliance had: living proof that vengeance and redemption could share a bloodline and choose, every day, which one to be. Lugdum made them central to the new Alliance, and they repaid it.

❦ ❧

So the lines were drawn for the last battle of the age. On one side, Sune and his ever-growing legion of the taken, certain to the last that he was the hero of his own correction. On the other, the widest Alliance the world had ever seen - Humans and Elves and Dwarves, and now Orcs and Half Orcs beside them, and Kushinadahime the Princess of Sekai at the center of it, holding the arch up as she had held it for a hundred years.

And beneath them all, in the sealed dark, Izanami watched her second weapon carry the war to the very gate of Yomi - and readied the only move she had been waiting a thousand years to make. Sune thought the war was his. The Alliance thought the war was theirs. Neither of them understood that the war had only ever been Izanami's, and that its true purpose was not to conquer Sekai at all. It was to bring the sword, and the Princess, and every people of the living world, to one place. To her door.
