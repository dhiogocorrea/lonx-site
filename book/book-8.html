Chapter VIII

The March to Yomi

Age of the Sword · Year 161

In the hundred-and-sixty-first year of the world, Kushinadahime did a thing that had never been done before and has not been done since. She gathered, into a single host, every people that walked the living world - and marched them all toward the one place the living had spent their entire history learning to avoid.

The gate of Yomi. The seam where Izanagi's stone still held back the dark. The border-place the Elves had feared correctly since the twelfth year of the world. For an age it had been a thing to keep away from. Now the Princess of Sekai led the whole of the living toward it on purpose, because the war with Sune had made one thing unbearably clear: the sickness in the world did not begin with the sword, or with the Orcs, or with any single wound. It began below. It had always begun below. And it would keep beginning, wound after wound, weapon after weapon, for as long as the thing in the dark was left alone to grieve and to hate and to whisper.

The host of all peoples

The chronicles linger on the sight of that gathered host, and this Book will linger too, because it is the last wholly hopeful image before the door opens. Consider who marched together to the gate of the dead:

The Humans came, of course - twice-burned, twice-rebuilt, their hunger for tomorrow turned at last toward the defense of the whole world rather than the raising of their own. The Elves came out of Yomotsu, the moonlit realm that had always kept the border, marching now toward the thing they had watched from a distance for a hundred and fifty years. The Dwarves came from Unshu - or from the ruin of it, for Sune had struck the island hard - carrying their engines and their shields and their grief, and Onamuji Firedredger at their head, older now, having buried more of his people than any commander should have to and still refusing to let the survivors turn back.

And behind the three founding peoples came the others, the ones no one would have believed possible a hundred years before. The Orcs marched under Lugdum - the race made of hatred, choosing the harder thing, walking toward the mother who had made them as if to say we are not yours, and we will prove it at your own door. The Half Orcs marched beside them, belonging to both sides of a war their very existence had helped to end. And most strangely of all, there marched Phantoms - some few of the taken who had begun, in the chaos of the war, to slip the sword's command. They did not fully understand what they were, or why they now fought against the legion they had been torn from. They only knew, in whatever was left of them, that they no longer wished to serve. That much of a soul the sword had failed to eat. It would prove to be enough.

Six peoples stood at the gate of the dead. Two of them had been created by the enemy they had come to face. That is not the failure of Izanami's design. It is its total defeat.

Sune at the portal

Sune came too, driving his legion before the Alliance, still certain he was winning, still certain the sword's voice and his own greatness were one and the same. He fought his way to the very gate of Yomi - to the seam, the thin place, the door - and there, at last, the sword told him what all its whispering had been for. It told him to open it. It told him that beyond the stone lay the final greatness, the last correction, the reward for everything he had suffered at the hands of a world too small for him. All he had to do was throw the blade through.

He believed it. He had believed everything else; why would he stop now, one step from the greatness the voice had promised since the forge? He raised LONX - the beautiful, terrible thing he had made, the mouth he had fed with a war's worth of souls - and with the last of his strength he threw it into the portal, into the dark, into Yomi itself.

The stone that no living hand could move was moved, at last, by the hand of a dead thing thrown by a living fool. The door of the world opened.

❦ ❧

What came through it was not the greatness Sune had been promised. What came through it was Izanami - or the reach of her, the will of her, a thousand years of grief and patience finally given a road into the light. The war stopped. Both armies, Alliance and legion alike, felt the temperature of the whole world drop, and understood together, too late, that neither of them had ever been the true player in this game.

Sune had opened the door. He had been the key all along - the ambitious heart, the forger's hunger, the hand that would carry the sword and feed it and, at the very end, throw it home. He had spent his whole life wanting to matter. In the last moment before Izanami discarded him, this Book allows him one small mercy: he finally did. He mattered enormously. He mattered as the worst mistake in the history of the world. It was not what he had wanted, but it was, at last, undeniable.
