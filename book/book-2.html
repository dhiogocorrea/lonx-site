Chapter II

Water and Light

Age of Gods · Year 3

Izanagi came up out of Yomi and the world did not recognize him. He had gone down a maker and come back a mourner, and worse - he had come back unclean. The dark of that country clung to him the way smoke clings to cloth, in the seams, in the breath, in places a hand cannot reach to brush it away.

He could feel it on him. Every step he took across the young world left a shadow that had not been there before. The grass where he stood grew a little colder. The birds went quiet as he passed. He understood, with the slow horror of a god learning something new, that he had carried a piece of death up into the land of the living, and that if he did not rid himself of it, everything he had made with Izanami would begin, quietly, to die of him.

The lake

So he went looking for water clean enough to wash a god. He found it at last in a lake so still and so clear that it had never held a reflection, because nothing had ever come close enough to cast one. He waded into it. And as the water of Sekai closed over the stain of Yomi, something happened that had never happened before: the world made something its makers had not planned.

For grief, it turns out, does not simply vanish when you wash it away. It changes hands. As the darkness lifted from Izanagi and dissolved into the divine water, the water could not simply hold it. Light and shadow met, and where they met, they quickened. Out of that lake rose three.

They were not made the way the world was made, with care and long conversation. They were born the way a storm is born - all at once, out of the meeting of two things that could not stay together.

The first to rise was Susanoo, and he came up out of the deep water with the sea already in him - its patience and its fury, its tides and its drowning storms. Where he breathed, the lake remembered it was connected to every ocean in the world, and the oceans answered.

The second was Amaterasu, and she rose into the daylight, and the daylight bent toward her as if it had been waiting. She was the sun given a face - not the distant fire in the sky, but its warmth, its ambition, its habit of making things grow whether they were ready or not.

The third rose last and quietest, in the moment between the sun on the water and the dark still bleeding out of Izanagi's skin. This was Tsukuyomi, god of the moon, of the long memory of night, of the cold clean light that does not warm you but does let you see. Of the three, he alone had touched the shadow of Yomi as he was born, and something of its patience stayed with him always.

These are the III Gods. The whole of Sekai's later worship, all its temples and festivals and holy days, all the enchantments a guild will one day carve into steel, trace back to that one moment at the lake - to a father washing his grief away and finding it had become his children.

The promise of small hands

Izanagi looked on what the lake had made and he was afraid, though he would not have called it that. He had loved the world too much, and lost it, and he could see already that these three had inherited his capacity for enormous feeling. Amaterasu wanted to make things grow. Susanoo wanted to move the seas. Tsukuyomi wanted, most dangerously of all, to understand. A world shaped by feelings that large would be a world shaped, over and over, by the same mistake that had cost him Izanami.

So before he stepped back from the affairs of the world - for a maker who has lost his other half does not stay long among the things they made together - Izanagi asked one thing of his children. Not obedience. Not worship. He asked them to keep their hands small.

Whatever they made, he told them, they must let it become its own. They could kindle the sun, hang the moon, raise the tides - but they must not reach down into the lives of what they created and steer them, however much they loved them, however clearly they saw the coming grief. To love a thing was to let it be free enough to break your heart. He knew. He had the underworld to prove it.

The III Gods promised. Amaterasu promised warmly, meaning it. Susanoo promised roughly, the way he did everything. Tsukuyomi promised last, and the chronicles note - because everything in this Book is a chronicle of promises and their prices - that when he promised, he was already looking north and east, toward the cold forests where he would one day set his own people beneath the moon.

And far below, sealed behind a stone no living hand could move, Izanami felt the world change. She could not see the lake. She could not see her husband's children rise dripping into a daylight she would never feel again. But she felt the warmth of them arrive in the world above her like a door opening in a house she had been thrown out of, and she understood that Sekai had gone on without her, and had even grown lovely in her absence.

She could not leave Yomi. That much was fixed. But she could watch. And she began, in the patient way of the very old and the very wounded, to learn how.
