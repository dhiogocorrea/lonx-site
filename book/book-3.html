Chapter III

The Peoples of Sekai

Age of Peoples · Years 12 - 24

The III Gods kept their father's promise for a while, as the young always do - which is to say, they kept the letter of it and quietly broke the spirit the moment it became inconvenient. They did not steer the lives of what they made. They simply could not resist making, and each of them made in their own image, and the world has been reckoning with the differences ever since.

The Elves, under the moon

Tsukuyomi made first, in the twelfth year of the world. He went into the oldest forests, where the trees had been growing since Izanagi and Izanami first dreamed of green things, and beneath the full moon he shaped a people to match the light he loved. He called them Elves, and he gave them the qualities of the night that is not dark: long sight, longer memory, quick feet, and a patience that could outlast stone.

He gave them Yomotsu, the moonlit forest realm, to keep - and here the chroniclers pause, because the name is not an accident. Yomotsu shares its root with Yomi. Whether Tsukuyomi named it in unknowing echo of the country that had touched him at his birth, or whether some part of him always meant to set his people as a watch upon the border of the dead, no one living can say. But of the three peoples, it was the Elves who first learned to feel the cold breath that leaks, on certain nights, from the sealed places of the world. They learned to fear it correctly. That fear would one day save Sekai, and it would also, through no fault of theirs, be turned into its greatest wound.

An Elf remembers everything. This is their glory in peace and their agony in war, for they cannot forgive the way the short-lived can - by simply forgetting.

The Dwarves, in the stone

Susanoo made next, in the sixteenth year, and he made nothing like his brother's graceful watchers. He went down to the island of Unshu, where the sea beat itself white against black rock, and he made a people as stubborn as the stone and as deep as the water. The Dwarves came into the world already arguing, already building, already certain that anything worth having was worth making twice as strong as it needed to be.

He gave them the crafts, all of them at once - the working of metal and the raising of ships, the cutting of stone and the brewing of drink and, not least, the making of songs, for Susanoo believed that a people who could not sing their own history would forget who they were the first hard winter. The Dwarves took to all of it. Unshu became a forge-island and a shipyard and a great stone hall of endless overlapping song, and the Dwarves grew rich and slow to trust and slower to leave their own shores, which is a fine way to live right up until the day the world comes to your door and will not be turned away.

The Humans, in the sun

Amaterasu made last, in the twenty-fourth year, and she made in her own restless likeness. She warmed a wide plain until it begged for something to grow there, and then she made the Humans - and where the Elves were given long life and the Dwarves deep craft, the Humans were given the one gift that would let them, in time, outrun both. She gave them hunger. Not for food; for more. More land, more knowledge, more tomorrow than yesterday had held.

They built Ashihara, the great human capital, faster than anyone thought a young people could build anything. They turned steel into tools and tools into cities and cities into a civilization, and they did it in a handful of generations, burning bright and short and passing everything they learned to children who would burn brighter. Of all the peoples, the Humans understood death best, because they met it soonest, and so they hurried - and their hurry made them, alone among the peoples, capable of changing what they were. It is why, of all the races who would one day climb the tower, the Humans alone can become nearly anything. They were made to.

Three peoples, one world

So Sekai was peopled. The Elves kept the forests and the border of night; the Dwarves kept the island and the deep craft; the Humans spread across the sun-warmed plains and multiplied and reached. For a long age they barely knew one another. An Elf might live a full mortal lifetime without once meeting a Human. A Dwarf might never in his life leave Unshu. Each people believed, in the comfortable way peoples do, that the world was mostly about them.

The III Gods watched their children flourish and were, for a season, content. They had made much and steered little. They had, they believed, kept the promise. And it is true that they had not reached down to move a single life against its will.

❦ ❧

But there was a fourth watcher, and she had made no promise at all. From the sealed dark below, Izanami looked up through the thin places of the world and counted what her husband's children had made. Three peoples. Three homelands. Three separate happinesses, each one a small warm light in a world that had been built, once, to keep her from ever being cold.

She could not reach them with her hands. Izanagi's stone still held. But she had spent long years learning the one road out of Yomi that no stone could seal - the road that runs through sleep, through longing, through the small resentments that every living heart carries and thinks no one else can see. She could not walk into the world. But she was learning, at last, how to whisper into it. And she had decided what her first whisper would be.

She would find the one among all these bright new peoples whose heart already held a door. And she would knock.
